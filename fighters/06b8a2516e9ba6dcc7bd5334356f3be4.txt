<p>Ricardo Almeida also known as Cachorrão is a top Welterweight MMA fighter for the UFC and an accomplished Black belt in Brazilian Jiu Jitsu who has medalled in some of the most important grappling tournaments in the World<br />
.</p>
<h2>Ricardo Almeida in Detail</h2>
<p><strong>Nickname:</strong> “Cachorrao” means “Big Dog” in Portuguese. The name came about before Ricardo even started training BJJ. As an avid Surfer, Ricardo Almeida had the reputation of kicking people out of the sea and being very protective of his surfing spot, because of this the local beaches’ lifeguards gave him the name “Cachorrao”, a famous soap opera character that resembled  a mobster<br />
.<br />
<strong>Lineage: </strong>Mitsuyo Maeda &gt; <a href="http://www.bjjheroes.com/bjj-fighters/carlos-gracie-sr-profile">Carlos Gracie</a> &gt; <a href="http://www.bjjheroes.com/bjj-fighters/helio-gracie">Helio Gracie</a> &gt; <a href="http://www.bjjheroes.com/bjj-fighters/carlos-carlinhos-gracie-jr">Carlos Gracie Jr</a> &gt; <a href="http://www.bjjheroes.com/bjj-fighters/renzo-gracie-bjj-fighter">Renzo Gracie</a> &gt; Ricardo Almeida<br />
.<br />
<strong>Main Achievements in BJJ/Grappling/MMA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>2x Brazilian National Champion (Medio &#8211; Purple Belt 1996, Pesado – Brown Belt 1997);</li>
<li>Pan American Champion (Pesado – Brown Belt 1999);</li>
<li>2x ADCC Silver Medallist (1998 88-98Kg and Openweight Divisions);</li>
<li>ADCC Bronze Medallist (2000 Openweight);</li>
<li>Former Pancrase Champion</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Weight Category in BJJ:</strong> Meio Pesado (88kg)<br />
.<br />
<strong>Favourite Technique:</strong> Guillotine<br />
.<br />
<strong>Team/Association: </strong>Renzo Gracie Jiu Jitsu</p>
<h3>Ricardo Almeida Biography</h3>
<p>Ricardo Almeida was born on the 29th of November 1976 in Weehawken, New Jersey (USA). He is the son of Brazilian Parents and lived in Brazil for most of his childhood and adolescence.</p>
<p>He started training BJJ when he was 17 years old, after a surfing friend invited him to come and train at Gracie Barra in Rio de Janeiro.</p>
<p>Training in the Barra he met and became friends with <a href="http://www.bjjheroes.com/bjj-fighters/renzo-gracie-bjj-fighter">Renzo Gracie</a> , with whom he moved on to New York to help establish his Brazilian Jiu Jitsu school there. Ricardo was awarded his black belt by the Gracie shortly after becoming the very first Black Belt awarded by Renzo Gracie.</p>
<p>He began his MMA career by chance, when he was in Japan cornering Ryan Gracie and one fighter on the event pulled out due to injury, as there were no late replacements available Ricardo was offered the chance to fight Akira Shoji (already a “made” fighter back then). Mr Almeida was not fearful and took the short notice fight, winning it by decision, it was December 2000.</p>
<p>In 2004, after a good run in the MMA scene, fighting in the biggest show’s at the time (Pride Fc, UFC and Pancrase) Ricardo Almeida decided to pull out and dedicate himself to his BJJ schools and his family. He sated that he was fighting alot in Japan and losing time to see his new born baby grow, so he decided to pull back at his prime.</p>
<p>On November 30, 2003, Almeida was involved in an altercation with middleweight opponent Nate Marquardt in a Pancrase title fight. Marquardt was put into a guillotine choke and tapped, Almeida did not disengage and the referee was unable to immediately separate the fighters. After the choke was let go, Marquardt threw a strike at Almeida&#8217;s face, prompting both corners to storm into the ring – including Renzo Gracie, who was cornering Almeida and kicked Marquardt in the face. The confrontation came to an end, and Marquardt approached Almeida after the bout to congratulate him. Almeida then vacated his title in July 2004.</p>
<p>In 2008 Cachorrao came back to MMA and is fighting now on the UFC. Although he always fought MMA in the Middleweight Division, he recently suffered an injury in his back which impairs Almeida from working any sort of weight lifting. This has made his natural weight drop substantially, making the welterweight division a more realistic challenge.</p>
